3. **Corporations that are partnerships or trusts in certain circumstances**: If a partnership or trust is deemed to be a corporation for tax purposes, it must file a T2 return.

Here's how the SBD works and how it reduces corporate tax:

1. **Eligibility**: The SBD is available to Canadian-controlled private corporations (CCPCs) that have a business income of $500,000 or less. The $500,000 limit is adjusted for inflation each year.

2. **Calculation**: The SBD is calculated as a percentage of the corporation's qualifying small business income (QSBI). For the 2023 taxation year, the percentage is 12.5%. The QSBI is the corporation's net income from active business operations, before certain deductions and additions.

3. **Application**: The SBD is applied by reducing the corporation's taxable income by the amount of the deduction. This effectively lowers the corporate tax rate for small businesses.

4. **Reduction in Corporate Tax**: The SBD can significantly reduce the corporate tax paid by small businesses. By lowering the taxable income, the corporation's overall tax burden is reduced. This means that small businesses can keep more of their profits to reinvest in their business, which can lead to increased growth and employment.

For example, if a small business has $100,000 in net income from active business operations, and it qualifies for the SBD, the deduction would be $12,500 (12.5% of $100,000). This would reduce the taxable income to $87,500, potentially lowering the corporate tax owed.

It's important to note that there are specific rules and limitations around the SBD, including restrictions on passive income and limitations on the deduction if the corporation has more than one related party shareholder. For detailed information and to determine eligibility, it is recommended to refer to the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) guidelines or consult a tax professional.

### 回答

Schedule 13, Continuity of Reserves, in the context of the Canadian corporate income tax is used to ensure that a corporation maintains a level of reserves in its financial statements that is consistent with the income tax rules. This schedule is part of the T2 Corporation Income Tax Guide and is designed to help corporations track and report the continuity of their reserves, which are amounts set aside from income for future expenses or investments.

Here are the main purposes of Schedule 13:

1. **Income Tax Compliance**: It ensures that the corporation's financial reserves are in compliance with the Income Tax Act. This helps in preventing corporations from manipulating their reserves to reduce their taxable income in one year and then reversing those reductions in subsequent years.

2. **Transparency**: It provides transparency in the corporation's financial reporting by revealing how reserves are being managed and utilized.

3. **Consistency**: It ensures consistency between the corporation's financial statements and its income tax return. This is important because the Income Tax Act has specific rules about when and how reserves can be created and released.

4. **Preventing Tax Evasion**: By requiring corporations to report the continuity of their reserves, the CRA (Canada Revenue Agency) can more effectively monitor and prevent tax evasion.

Schedule 13 requires corporations to report information such as the opening balance of reserves, the amount added to reserves during the year, the amount released from reserves, and the closing balance of reserves. It also requires explanations for any significant changes in reserves.
